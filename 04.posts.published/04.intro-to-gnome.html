The "Super Key"

It wouldn't seem right to call the Windows key something that referred to a operating system and brand you are not using, so in GNOME we have the Super key. While most keyboards have a left and right Super key, it is the right Super that is used to switch between the overview and desktop. This can be changed to the right Supper using the Tweak Tool which you will be introduced to below.

Window Minimize & Maximize Buttons

Before continuing on with the introduction there is one thing I think you may already have noticed and want to change in the GNOME UI: Where on earth did the window minimize and maximize buttons go? Why they are turned-off is a mystery to me. Fortunately, they are easy to turn on.

Press Super, then type 'tweak'. When you see the 'Tweak Tool' appear click on to launch it.

Click on the Window tab and turn minimize and maximize on as shown in the picture.

Lock Screen

To move from the lock screen to the login screen press Space or Enter.

Desktop

As mentioned, there isn't much to see on the Desktop. The GNOME desktop is rather stark. This keeps the focus on the applications/windows you are working in. If you want to jazz it up some, GNOME is highly customizable, as you will see below when we discuss GNOME Shell Extensions With shell extensions, you can introduce many elements that may be familiar to you from other operating systems.

Top Bar

There are 3 main areas of the top bar.

  1. Pressing Super or clicking the button on the left side of the top bar brings-up the Overview which is described below.
  2. In the middle of the top bar is the day and time. Clicking on it brings-up the notification area and a calendar. Many applications post notifications here including calendar appointments.
  3. On the right side of the top bar is a group of icons for network, sound and power. Clicking on any of them brings-up the system menu which we take a closer look at later.

Overview

Pressing the Super or clicking the button key brings up the Overview

The vertical bar on the left is called the Dash and is used to launch applications. Clicking the grid button brings up installed applications. There are two buttons at the bottom of the page, and , which do what they say. You can navigate through the pages of applications via keyboard or mouse.

On the right side are the workspaces. When you first bring-up the overview they appear as a narrow vertical bar. To expand the bar as shown in the picture, move you mouse to the right side of the screen.

When the overview is active typing will begin a search which will show installed applications as well as applications available in the Software store.

To me, this is the best way to launch an application. For example, pressing Super followed by typing 'write' and then pressing press Enter will launch LibreOffice Writer without searching through menus or even reaching for you mouse.

Alt-Tab

Pressing Alt-Tab brings-up the application switcher. For applications that have more than one windows open pressing Alt-~ cycles through its windows. In a later post I'll show you how to un-group the application windows in the event you find that easier as I do.

System Menu

As mentioned, clicking on any of the buttons at the far right of the Top Bar opens the system menu. This is where you go to connect to a wireless network. My machine does not have wireless show it is not showing in the picture.
